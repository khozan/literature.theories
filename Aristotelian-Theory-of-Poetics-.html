The theories of literature


The Aristotelian Theory of Poetics, formulated by the ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle around 350 BC, is one of the most influential and enduring theories in the field of literature. Aristotle's work on poetics is primarily found in his treatise called "Poetics."

History:
Aristotle's "Poetics" is a collection of his observations and analyses on the nature of poetry and drama. Although the exact date of its composition is uncertain, it is believed to have been written during Aristotle's time spent at the Lyceum in Athens, where he taught philosophy to his followers. The treatise provides a comprehensive understanding of Greek tragedy and epic poetry, encompassing themes, characters, plot structure, and the use of language, among other aspects.

Explanation of the Theory:
The Aristotelian Theory of Poetics primarily focuses on tragedy, considering it as the highest form of poetry. Aristotle believed that tragedy is an imitation (mimesis) of an action that evokes fear and pity in the audience, leading to catharsis, which is a purgation of emotions.
According to Aristotle, a tragic drama consists of six main elements:
1. Plot (Mythos): Aristotle emphasizes that the plot is the most important element of a tragedy. He argues that a well-structured plot should have a beginning, middle, and end, with a clear cause-and-effect chain of events. The plot should evoke emotions and have a unified and logical structure.
2. Character (Ethos): Aristotle emphasizes the importance of character development in a tragedy. He states that the characters should be realistic, have moral qualities that are appropriate for their roles, and undergo changes throughout the play.
3. Thought (Dianoia): This element refers to the themes, ideas, and intellectual content conveyed in a tragedy. Aristotle suggests that a tragedy should explore universal human experiences and address profound questions of life, morality, and society.
4. Diction (Lexis): This element concerns the choice and use of language in a tragedy. Aristotle insists that the language should be appropriate for the characters and the situation, employing poetic language and harmony.
5. Spectacle (Opsis): Aristotle recognizes the significance of visual and auditory elements in a tragedy. Spectacle includes the costumes, stage design, music, and other visual effects. However, Aristotle argues that spectacle should not overshadow the importance of the plot and characters.

6. Melody (Melos): This element pertains to the musical components in tragedy, including the use of chorus and vocal expression. Aristotle suggests that melody should enhance the emotional impact of the play.
In addition to the elements of tragedy, Aristotle discusses various concepts, such as the concept of the tragic hero, the role of catharsis, and the distinction between tragedy and epic poetry.

The Importance and Impact:
The Aristotelian Theory of Poetics had a profound influence on the development of Western literary theory and criticism. It provided a systematic framework for analyzing and understanding dramatic literature, and its principles were widely adopted and adapted by subsequent scholars and playwrights throughout history.
Aristotle's theories on plot structure, character development, and the purpose of tragedy continue to shape our understanding and appreciation of literature in various forms. Despite being more than two millennia old, the Aristotelian Theory of Poetics remains a fundamental text in literary studies and continues to be studied and interpreted by scholars and enthusiasts worldwide.

Example of Applying Aristotelian Theory of Poetics:
 To illustrate the use of Aristotelian Theory of Poetics in analyzing a poem in English, we can use the example of "The Raven" by Edgar Allan Poe.
1. Plot (Mythos): In "The Raven," the plot focuses on the main narrative of a man grieving the loss of his beloved and facing despair. The plot follows the Aristotelian structure, introducing the initial meaning (exposition) and developing the conflict and feelings of sadness through the dialogue between the man and the raven.
2. Character (Ethos): The poem has one main character, the man narrating his story. He is depicted as a sorrowful, desperate man, obsessed with his loss. His character evolves from the initial stages of dealing with his grief to becoming increasingly insane and consumed by pain.
3. Thought (Dianoia): The main themes in the poem are grief, loss, and loneliness. The poem also explores other themes such as immortality and the relationship between humans and the supernatural world. Through the thoughts presented in the poem, such as philosophical questions about life and death, the poem stimulates the audience to ponder deeper questions.
4. Diction (Lexis): The language used in "The Raven" is melancholic, dark, and poetic. Poe employs words that portray sorrow and despair, as well as presents rhythmic patterns and repetition to create a musical element within the poem.
5. Spectacle (Opsis): As this poem is written in the form of written text, there are no visual aspects present in theatrical or dramatic performance. However, Poe's skillful use of language and imagery creates a contrasting atmosphere and allows the reader to imagine an eerie setting through descriptive environments.
6. Melody (Melos): Although the poem does not have explicit musical elements, the use of language and the rhythms present within it create a melodic and emotional ambiance when reading it.
In addition to analyzing the elements of Aristotelian Theory of Poetics, we can also apply other concepts mentioned in the theory, such as identifying tragic characters and considering the influence of catharsis evoked by the poem.
Applying Aristotelian Theory of Poetics in analyzing poetry helps us understand and appreciate the beauty of poetry in terms of its structure, themes, and the emotional impact it can evoke.
