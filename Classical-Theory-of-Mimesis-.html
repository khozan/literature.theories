The theories of literature


The Classical Theory of Mimesis, also known as the Theory of Imitation, originated in ancient Greece in the 4th century BC and was primarily formulated by philosophers such as Plato and Aristotle. This theory aimed to understand and explain the role of art, particularly literature and drama, in society.
In this theory, mimesis refers to the act of imitation or representation of the natural world in art. The philosophers believed that art should imitate and represent reality, and that it could provide insight into the human condition. They argued that art has the power to evoke emotions, reveal truths about life, and transfer knowledge and moral lessons.
Aristotle, in his work "Poetics," developed the Classical Theory of Mimesis further. He believed that art, specifically tragedy, imitates human actions and emotions by creating characters and situations that are relatable and recognizable to the audience. According to Aristotle, the purpose of art is to generate a cathartic experience, allowing the audience to experience emotions and learn from observing the consequences of the characters' actions.
To analyze a literary work using the Classical Theory of Mimesis, we can consider the following elements:
1. Imitation: Identifying how the work imitates or represents reality and whether it accurately reflects the human condition.
2. Characters: Analyzing whether the characters in the work are relatable and if their actions and emotions imitate real human behavior.
3. Plot: Examining the sequence of events in the work and assessing whether it follows a logical progression, imitating the cause-and-effect structure of real-life situations.
4. Emotions: Analyzing the emotional responses evoked by the work and considering whether they align with the theory's aim of generating catharsis.
5. Moral Lessons: Evaluating whether the work conveys moral lessons or insights into the human condition, as the theory emphasizes the didactic value of art.
To provide a specific example of the application of the Classical Theory of Mimesis in literary analysis, we can look at William Shakespeare's tragedy, "Macbeth." In this play, the characters, such as Macbeth and Lady Macbeth, imitate the ambitious nature and moral corruption that can exist in humanity. The plot imitates a tragic downfall, following the Aristotelian structure of a tragic hero's rise and eventual fall. The play evokes intense emotions, such as guilt and remorse, in the audience, providing a cathartic experience. Furthermore, "Macbeth" explores themes of the consequences of unchecked ambition and the corrupting influence of power, offering moral lessons and insights into the human condition.
In conclusion, the Classical Theory of Mimesis or the Theory of Imitation is a concept that originated in ancient Greece to explain the role of art, mainly literature and drama, in society. It emphasizes the imitation and representation of reality and the power of art to evoke emotions and reveal truths about life. By analyzing the elements of imitation, characters, plot, emotions, and moral lessons in a literary work, we can apply the Classical Theory of Mimesis to gain a deeper understanding of its artistic value and impact.
