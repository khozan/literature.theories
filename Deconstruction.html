The theories of literature


Deconstruction is a literary theory that emerged in the 1980s and is still widely discussed and studied in the present day. It originated from the work of the French philosopher Jacques Derrida, who challenged traditional literary and philosophical notions by deconstructing them.
History:
Deconstruction began as a critique of structuralism, a theory that emphasized the existence of universal structures in language, literature, and culture. Jacques Derrida criticized structuralism for disregarding the individual interpretation and multiple meanings that each text could have. He argued that language is inherently unstable and that it is impossible to find a fixed and definitive meaning in any written work.
Figures:
The main figure associated with deconstruction is Jacques Derrida. He was a French philosopher, known for his influential works such as "Of Grammatology" and "Writing and Difference". Derrida's ideas challenged the Western philosophical tradition and had a profound impact on literary theory.

Explanation:
Deconstruction aims to expose the underlying assumptions and contradictions present in texts. According to Derrida, every text contains binary oppositions and hierarchical structures that privilege certain meanings over others. Deconstruction seeks to destabilize these hierarchies and reveal the plurality of meanings that coexist within a text.
Deconstruction also focuses on the notion of undecidability, which suggests that no concept or meaning can be fully determined or defined. Derrida argued that traditional theories tend to rely on binary oppositions, such as good vs. evil or presence vs. absence. However, deconstruction emphasizes the interplay and inseparability of these opposites, blurring the boundaries between them.

Key concepts Theories:
The main idea behind deconstruction in literature is to challenge the fixed meanings and interpretations that readers often assign to texts. Deconstructionists argue that readers bring their own biases, ideologies, and preconceived notions when interpreting a text. Thus, a text can have multiple interpretations that depend on the reader's perspective and context.

In summary, deconstruction emerged in the 1980s as a philosophical and literary theory that challenges traditional notions of meaning, interpretation, and language. Jacques Derrida's ideas have had a lasting impact on the field of literary criticism, encouraging readers to question and examine the underlying assumptions and contradictions in texts.
