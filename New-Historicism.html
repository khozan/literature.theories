The theories of literature


New Historicism is a literary theory that emerged in the late 1980s and is still widely studied and applied in literary and cultural studies today. As the name suggests, it combines elements of history and literary analysis to provide a new perspective on literary texts.
History:
New Historicism developed as a response to the limitations of the New Criticism approach, which focuses solely on the text itself, disregarding the historical, social, and political context in which it was created. Scholars such as Michel Foucault, Clifford Geertz, and Louis Althusser greatly influenced the development of New Historicism. These thinkers emphasized the importance of understanding the text in relation to the historical period and cultural beliefs that shaped it.
Figures:
- Michel Foucault: A French philosopher whose ideas heavily influenced the field of New Historicism. Foucault believed that power is not confined to political institutions but is present in all aspects of society, including literature.
- Clifford Geertz: An American anthropologist known for his work on cultural interpretations. His concept of "thick description" emphasizes the importance of analyzing cultural context to fully understand texts.
- Louis Althusser: A French Marxist philosopher who contributed to the field of New Historicism by highlighting the role of ideology in shaping literary works.
Explanation:
New Historicism argues that literary texts are products of their time and are shaped by the historical and cultural context in which they are produced. It rejects the notion of objective truth and instead focuses on multiple interpretations and viewpoints.
This theory explores how power dynamics and cultural practices influence and are reflected in literary works. It aims to reveal the various power struggles, marginalized voices, and social hierarchies present in texts. New Historicism rejects the distinction between high and low literature, treating all forms of cultural production as worthy of analysis.
New Historicism also recognizes that literary texts themselves have the power to influence society and shape historical events. Therefore, it emphasizes the reciprocal relationship between literature and history. By examining how a text reflects and challenges the dominant ideologies of its time, scholars can gain a deeper understanding of the social and political issues of that period.
Tenets of Literary Theory:
1. The text is not evaluated in isolation but is analyzed in relation to the historical and cultural context in which it was produced.
2. Knowledge and power are interconnected. New Historicism aims to uncover power dynamics within texts and how they reflect the struggles of different social groups.
3. Literature has the ability to shape history. By examining how texts engage with societal issues, scholars can understand the impact of literature on shaping collective memory and historical events.
Overall, New Historicism provides a comprehensive approach to literary analysis by considering the historical and cultural factors that shape texts. It offers a new way of understanding literature as an influential force that both reflects and shapes society.

Example:
One example of using New Historicism to analyze a literary work is by examining George Orwell's novel "1984." New Historicism emerged in the late 1980s and focuses on understanding literature by considering the historical and cultural context in which it was produced.
When analyzing "1984" using this approach, we can examine how the novel reflects the anxieties and issues prevalent in Orwell's own time. Written in the aftermath of World War II and during the rise of totalitarian regimes, Orwell provides a dystopian vision of a future society controlled by a pervasive and oppressive government.
By applying New Historicism, we can explore how Orwell's own experiences during the war and his observations of authoritarian regimes influenced his depiction of a surveillance state in "1984." We can also consider how the political and social events of the late 1940s, such as the Cold War and the intensifying power struggles between superpowers, might have influenced Orwell's portrayal of a world divided into three warring factions.
Another aspect that can be analyzed using New Historicism is the novel's exploration of language and thought control. Orwell's critique of totalitarianism can be linked to the historical context of propaganda and manipulation of information during the war and the rise of mass media in the post-war era. By understanding these historical influences, we gain a deeper insight into Orwell's intentions and the broader cultural implications of his work.
Furthermore, New Historicism allows us to consider the reception and impact of "1984" in present times. We can analyze how the novel has been interpreted and appropriated in different cultural and political contexts, contributing to ongoing debates on surveillance, government control, and individual freedoms.
In conclusion, using New Historicism to analyze George Orwell's "1984" allows us to explore the historical, cultural, and political influences that shaped the novel, as well as its continued relevance in contemporary society. By situating the work within its specific historical context, we gain a richer understanding of the text and its implications.
