The theories of literature


Postcolonial Theory is a literary and cultural theory that emerged in the 1980s and continues to be influential today. It examines the social, political, and cultural effects of colonization and the experiences of colonized people in the aftermath of imperialism. This theory focuses on the relationship between colonizer and colonized, exploring the ways in which colonial powers exerted control and dominance over colonized societies.
Some key figures in the development of Postcolonial Theory include Edward Said, Homi K. Bhabha, Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak, and Frantz Fanon. Edward Said's book "Orientalism" (1978) is considered a foundational text in the field. He argued that Western representations of the East were created to justify and perpetuate colonial dominance. Homi K. Bhabha's concept of "hybridity" examines the cultural exchange that occurs in colonial encounters. Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak's work explores the subaltern, or marginalized, voices that are silenced by colonial power structures. Frantz Fanon's writings, such as "The Wretched of the Earth" (1961), analyze the psychological and existential impact of colonialism on colonized individuals.
Postcolonial Theory seeks to deconstruct and challenge traditional Western narratives and perspectives by giving voice to the experiences and perspectives of the colonized. It explores themes of identity, race, imperialism, power, and resistance in literature and other cultural forms.
The central tenets of Postcolonial Theory include an emphasis on the intersections of race, class, gender, and nationality, and an exploration of how power operates within colonial and postcolonial contexts. This theory seeks to dismantle colonial discourses and structures of power that have perpetuated inequality and marginalization.
Postcolonial literary analysis often focuses on texts from former colonies or works produced by authors from marginalized communities. It examines how colonized people represent their experiences, challenge dominant narratives, and express their cultural identities and histories.
Additionally, Postcolonial Theory can be used to analyze the legacy of colonialism in contemporary society, including issues of neocolonialism, cultural appropriation, and structural inequality. It provides a framework for understanding power dynamics and social hierarchies in a global context.
In summary, Postcolonial Theory is a critical lens that examines the effects of colonization and the experiences of the colonized. It challenges dominant narratives, explores power dynamics, and gives voice to marginalized communities in literature and other cultural forms.

Okonkwo, as the protagonist, can be seen as an example of an individual's struggle to preserve their identity and traditional culture amidst colonial domination. Through this character, Achebe can highlight identity politics, cultural conflicts, and the psychological effects of colonialism.
Furthermore, Postcolonial Theory can be applied to Achebe's writing style, including the use of language and narration. In this novel, Achebe creatively uses the English language to reflect the expression of Ibo culture and to depict the struggles of communication between Ibo and colonial cultures.
Overall, using Postcolonial Theory to analyze the novel "Things Fall Apart" enables us to understand how colonialism affects the lives of individuals, communities, and cultures. By examining this literary work through the lens of this theory, we can understand and critique the dynamics of power, culture, and resistance in the context of colonial and postcolonial times.
