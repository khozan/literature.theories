The theories of literature


Here is a detailed explanation of Structuralism as a literary theory that emerged in the late 1960s and continued into the early 1970s:
History:
Structuralism is a theoretical framework that originated in the field of linguistics and later expanded to other disciplines, including literature, anthropology, and philosophy. It emerged as a reaction to the dominant psychological and existentialist theories prevalent at the time. Structuralism was heavily influenced by the work of Swiss linguist Ferdinand de Saussure and the Prague School of Linguistics.
Explanation:
Structuralism approaches literature by examining the underlying structures and systems that give meaning to a text. It aims to uncover the deep structures that shape the surface manifestations of language and meaning. In this approach, the text is seen as a network of interconnected elements rather than a collection of individual symbols or words.
Key Concepts:
1. Signifier and Signified: Structuralism emphasizes the distinction between the signifier (a linguistic or visual symbol) and the signified (the concept or meaning associated with that symbol). According to Saussure, the relationship between the signifier and signified is arbitrary and is determined by the conventions of a given linguistic system.
2. Binary Oppositions: Structuralists analyze the text by identifying binary oppositions, or contrasting pairs of concepts that define each other. For example, light vs. dark, good vs. evil. These oppositions create meaning and structure within the text and are seen as fundamental to understanding it.
3. Structures and Systems: Structuralists focus on the underlying structures and systems that organize and shape a text. They seek to uncover the underlying rules and patterns that create meaning. These structures can be linguistic, narrative, social, or cultural.
4. Interdisciplinarity: Structuralism emphasizes interdisciplinary approaches, drawing on various fields like linguistics, anthropology, and philosophy. This interdisciplinary approach allows for a deeper understanding of the complex relationships between language, culture, and society.
Criticism and Decline:
Structuralism faced criticism for its tendency to oversimplify texts and reduce them to underlying structures. Critics argued that it neglected the individuality of authors and readers and disregarded historical and cultural contexts. Additionally, the rigid methodology and abstract nature of structuralist analysis made it challenging to apply to all types of texts.
By the late 1970s, structuralism began to decline in popularity as other theories such as post-structuralism and postmodernism emerged, which sought to critique and move beyond the limits of structuralism.
Overall, Structuralism was an influential theory that approached literature by analyzing the underlying structures and systems that give meaning to a text. While its popularity declined, it paved the way for subsequent literary theories and methodologies.

For example, the signifier "blood" is used throughout the play, both literally and symbolically. The signified for blood can represent guilt, violence, and power. 2. Binary Oppositions: We can identify several binary oppositions in "Macbeth," such as good vs. evil, ambition vs. morality, and appearance vs. reality. These opposing pairs create tension and drive the plot of the play. By closely analyzing these binary oppositions and their interplay, we can uncover the deeper structures that give meaning to the text.
3. Structures and Systems: Structuralism encourages us to examine the structures and systems within "Macbeth." For example, we can analyze the dramatic structure of the play and how it shapes the narrative and character development. We can also examine the hierarchical structure of power and its effects on the characters. By studying these structures, we can gain a deeper understanding of the play's themes and intentions.
4. Interdisciplinarity: To fully analyze "Macbeth" using structuralism, we can draw upon various disciplines. For instance, we can explore the linguistic structures in Shakespeare's language and the cultural context in which he wrote. We can also draw on anthropology to examine the social structures and power dynamics portrayed in the play. By incorporating insights from different disciplines, we can develop a more comprehensive analysis of the text.
By employing the principles of structuralism, we can analyze "Macbeth" by examining its underlying structures, binary oppositions, and the relationships between signifiers and signifieds. Through this analysis, we gain a deeper understanding of the play and the intricate interplay of its themes and meanings.
